The Challenge

Virtual Vendors is an online platform designed to empower street vendors and small business owners by helping them quickly set up digital storefronts. Many vendors, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic, lacked the technical knowledge or resources to transition their businesses online.

The Solution

The goal of the platform was to provide an intuitive, easy-to-use solution that enabled vendors to manage their inventory, interact with customers, and increase sales digitally—all within the constraints of a 24-hour hackathon.

Understanding the Problem

Background

Each semester, universities like Rutgers host Involvement Fairs for students to explore organizations. However, with over 800 organizations available and each using its own platform for communication (like GroupMe, Discord, and Instagram), students find it challenging to stay informed. This issue became worse during the pandemic when many activities went virtual, leading to a loss of engagement.

Motivation

Students and coordinators use various communication tools—Google Classroom, Slack, Discord, GroupMe, Zoom—causing confusion and inefficiencies. Important information often gets lost across different platforms, and managing multiple organizations becomes overwhelming. There was a clear need for a unified platform to streamline communication and organization management.

Design Process

Empathize & Define

To understand the needs of students, I conducted a survey and interviews with 10 current college students and recent graduates.

Key Findings

  • 100% of participants used social media (GroupMe, Facebook, etc.) to communicate with organizations.
  • 50% relied solely on email.
  • 90% learned about clubs through word-of-mouth.
  • 55% discovered opportunities via social media and email.

Pain Points

  • Poor communication from organization coordinators, especially in larger groups.
  • Lack of consistent promotion of events and opportunities.
  • Difficulty in managing multiple platforms for communication.

User Personas

To ensure the app catered to diverse student needs, I created three key personas:

  • Jasmine, 18, a Rutgers freshman on the pre-med track, who struggles with the virtual transition and wants to meet new people.
  • Eric, 21, a senior in Computer Science and a club coordinator who seeks a streamlined way to coordinate meetings and share information with members.
  • Jessica, 20, a junior who started college during the pandemic and finds it difficult to discover clubs and events.

Ideation: Translating User Insights into Features

The ideation process began after synthesizing insights from user research. Based on the key pain points and needs identified, I brainstormed a set of core features that would address the challenges faced by students and organization leaders.

Key Priorities

  • Centralized communication: A space where students can easily communicate with organizations and receive updates in one place.
  • Event discovery: An intuitive search feature where students can explore organizations and upcoming events based on their interests.
  • Task management: A system to help students track and complete tasks assigned within organizations, reducing the complexity of managing responsibilities.
  • Personalized dashboard: A quick-access hub where students can view their memberships, tasks, and upcoming events, promoting ease of use.
  • Profile creation: A feature for students to create personal profiles, showcasing their interests and organization affiliations for more tailored connections.

The ideation process was visualized using mind maps and user flows to outline how these features would work together to form a cohesive app. I drew inspiration from productivity tools like Trello for task management and social platforms like LinkedIn and Facebook for community engagement. My goal was to integrate the social, organizational, and management aspects seamlessly.

Wireframing: Structuring the User Experience

With a clear idea of the app’s core features, I proceeded to the wireframing phase, where I laid out the structure and flow of the user experience. Wireframes helped me visualize how each feature would be organized, ensuring ease of navigation and prioritizing essential user actions.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

After validating the low-fidelity wireframes through peer feedback, I moved to mid-fidelity wireframes, introducing more detailed elements like icons, navigation patterns, and placeholder content.

  • Objective: Test how well the layout supports user goals and ensure information is intuitively presented.
  • Enhancements Made:
    • Adjusted navigation menus to make them more prominent.
    • Refined the hierarchy of information in the Dashboard, prioritizing tasks over less time-sensitive items like messages.
    • Streamlined the Explore tab by adding filter options to make searching for organizations more precise and user-friendly.

At this stage, I began testing the wireframes with potential users, asking them to perform basic tasks like finding a specific event or checking tasks. The feedback gathered was invaluable in fine-tuning the layout and ensuring an optimal user experience.

Explore & Support Vendors

  • Description: Customers can explore trending and upcoming vendor events, providing visibility to vendors. This functionality helps connect vendors with community events where they can gain more exposure.
  • UI Elements: A card-based interface showcases events and vendors, ensuring a user-friendly browsing experience.

Vendor Profiles & Product Listings:

  • Description: Vendors can create a profile, upload products, and update inventory seamlessly. The platform is designed to accommodate product images and descriptions, making it easy for vendors to showcase their offerings.

Challenges & Solutions

Throughout the design and development process of the AfterSchool app, several challenges arose, requiring thoughtful solutions to ensure the final product met user needs effectively.

Challenge 1: Balancing Simplicity with Functionalitys

During the ideation phase, it became clear that packing too many features into the app risked overwhelming users. To address this, I focused on prioritizing core features that students would find most valuable, such as task management, communication, and event discovery. Additional features were strategically placed to be secondary, accessed only when needed. This balance maintained the app’s usability without compromising its utility.

Challenge 2: Creating a Unified Experience for Different User Types

The target users—students and organization leaders—had different needs. For students, ease of navigation and communication was critical, while for organization leaders, managing events and tasks was a priority. By designing separate yet interconnected dashboards for each group, I provided tailored functionality without fragmenting the overall experience.

Challenge 3: Maintaining Engagement and User Retention

To foster ongoing engagement, I incorporated gamification elements such as progress bars for task completion and notifications for upcoming events. This approach created a sense of achievement and urgency, encouraging students to regularly check the app and remain active in their organizations.

Results

The project resulted in a fully functioning high-fidelity prototype for AfterSchool, with positive outcomes validated through usability testing and feedback.

Outcomes

The AfterSchool app successfully met user needs through a clean, user-centered design that improved task management, event discovery, and communication for students and organization leaders. Early usability tests showed increased productivity and engagement as students could better organize their extracurricular activities in one central app. The app’s intuitive interface for task assignment and event creation also streamlined workflows for leaders. The prototype's success generated interest from university stakeholders, leading to plans for further development and potential funding to move toward an MVP phase.

Key Takeaways

  • User Research Drives Success: Conducting in-depth interviews and focus groups early in the process ensured that the final design aligned with user needs, reducing the need for significant rework.
  • Iterative Design is Crucial: The project benefited immensely from multiple rounds of wireframing and prototyping. Testing both low- and high-fidelity wireframes allowed me to catch usability issues early, leading to a smoother final design process.
  • Balancing Features is Key: Avoiding feature bloat by prioritizing core functionality kept the app simple yet effective. It’s important to resist the temptation to over-complicate the user experience, even if additional features may seem beneficial.

Conclusion

AfterSchool provided a comprehensive solution to the challenges faced by students managing their extracurricular activities. By focusing on user-centered design principles, I was able to create an intuitive, functional app that simplified communication, task management, and event discovery for both students and organization leaders. The iterative design process, informed by user feedback, led to a polished, high-fidelity prototype that is ready for potential development.

This project was an invaluable learning experience, demonstrating the importance of thorough research, collaborative feedback, and iterative design in creating digital products that make a meaningful impact on users' lives. Looking forward, I am excited to see how the app evolves into a functional product that empowers students to stay organized, engaged, and connected within their academic communities.
