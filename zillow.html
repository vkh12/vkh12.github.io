Overview

Most apartment search tools are built for individual decisionmaking, but homes are often shared.

This project explored how Zillow could better support roommates during apartment searching. While existing platforms make it easy to filter listings, they provide limited support for comparing different priorities, discussing tradeoffs, and reaching a shared decision.

My team and I redesigned the apartment search experience around shared preferences, group compatibility, and collaborative listing review so roommates could search, compare, and decide together in one place.

Problem Overview

Roommates struggle to align on a decision because the search process is fragmented across tools.

Instead of making decisions directly inside Zillow, roommates often rely on screenshots, group texts, spreadsheets, separate saved lists, and verbal conversations. This creates extra coordination work and makes it harder to understand which apartments satisfy the group as a whole.

Suggested image: a current-state workflow showing how roommate groups coordinate across separate tools.

How might we

help roommate groups search, compare, and decide on apartments together within one platform?

Our Solution

A collaborative apartment search experience.

The redesigned Zillow experience introduces a shared search space where roommates can set individual preferences, view group compatibility, and discuss listings together before making a decision.

01

Shared roommate preference

Each roommate can define priorities such as budget, commute, neighborhood, safety, amenities, pet needs, and lifestyle preferences.

02

Group compatibility score

Listings are ranked by how well they satisfy the group's combined preferences, helping roommates understand tradeoffs more clearly.

03

Collaborative listing review

Roommates can save listings, leave comments, compare tradeoffs, and vote on apartments in one shared decision space.

Key insight

Designing for groups means designing for negotiation, not just discovery.

A strong apartment search experience should not only return relevant listings. It should help people understand why a listing works, who it works for, and what tradeoffs still need to be discussed.

Reflection

This project helped me understand that relevance is not always individual. In collaborative decisions, the best result is the one that supports alignment, compromise, and trust.
